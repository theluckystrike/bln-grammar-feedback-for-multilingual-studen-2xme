How I Give Grammar Feedback to Multilingual Writers

Originally published at belikenative.com/grammar-feedback-for-multilingual-students

Most grammar mistakes my multilingual students make aren't random. They follow patterns tied directly to their first language, and once I started seeing errors that way, my feedback got a lot sharper. It took me a while to figure that out, though.

The Errors Aren't What You'd Expect

I used to mark every mistake the same way, red pen, correction, move on. But after working with students from about a dozen language backgrounds, I noticed recurring patterns that changed how I approached feedback entirely. My Spanish-speaking students kept adding articles where they didn't belong, writing things like "the my house is big." Mandarin-speaking students dropped articles entirely. And my Arabic-speaking students rearranged word order in ways that mapped perfectly onto Arabic sentence structure.

These aren't careless mistakes. They're direct transfers from how grammar works in the student's native language. A Japanese speaker might write "many informations" because Japanese doesn't pluralize nouns the same way English does. A Korean speaker might put verbs at the end of sentences because that's standard Korean syntax. Once I mapped out these patterns for each language group, I stopped treating all errors the same.

Fewer Corrections Work Better

I made a mistake early on by returning papers covered in corrections, thinking thoroughness was helpful. Turns out, it was the opposite. Students would glance at all that red ink and shut down, so I started limiting myself to two or three error types per assignment.

If a student was struggling with verb tenses, I'd focus only on that and leave preposition errors for the next draft. The result surprised me. Students retained corrections at a much higher rate when they weren't drowning in feedback, and the quality of their revisions improved noticeably between drafts.

For beginners, I give direct corrections with a brief explanation of the rule. "She go to school" becomes "She goes to school" with a short note about third-person singular verbs. That's straightforward and it works at that level. For more advanced students, I switched to indirect feedback, underlining the error or using a code like "SV" for subject-verb agreement and letting them figure out the fix. That approach pushed them to internalize the rules instead of just copying my corrections.

What to Fix First

Not all grammar errors carry the same weight. Some make a sentence unreadable, while others are minor quirks that don't really affect meaning.

I prioritize errors that block communication. Wrong verb tenses can completely change what a sentence means, so those get addressed immediately in my feedback. Preposition mistakes usually cause partial confusion, so they come next. Article errors rarely lead to real misunderstanding, so I save those for later revisions. This ranking system keeps my feedback focused, and students know exactly what to work on without feeling like everything they wrote was wrong.

Adjusting by Skill Level

My beginner students need scaffolding, so I teach one grammar concept at a time, starting with simple present tense before touching continuous forms. Each correction comes with a brief explanation and an example they can reference later. The pace matters as much as the content at this stage.

Intermediate students get more independence. I'll point out error categories without giving the fix, then ask them to revise on their own. At this stage, they're ready to recognize patterns and self-correct with some guidance. The shift from direct to indirect feedback usually happens around the B1 level on the CEFR scale, at least in my experience.

Advanced students are a different situation entirely. Their grammar is mostly solid, so feedback shifts toward style, tone, and word choice. I encourage them to experiment with sentence structure and voice, because the goal isn't just correctness anymore, it's fluency.
